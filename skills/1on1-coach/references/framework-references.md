Canonical reference for all frameworks, models, and theory used in the 1on1 analysis and coaching prompts.

## 1on1 Best Practices

| Source | Author | URL |
|---|---|---|
| "The Art of the Awkward 1:1" | Mark Rabkin (Meta) | https://medium.com/@mrabkin/the-art-of-the-awkward-1-1-f4e1dcbd1c5c |
| "Awkward 1:1s: The Art of Getting Honest Feedback" | Mark Rabkin | https://medium.com/@mrabkin/awkward-1-1s-the-art-of-getting-honest-feedback-2843078b2880 |
| "El role del Engineering Manager: One on Ones" | Felipe López Luis | https://flopezluis.medium.com/el-role-del-engineering-manager-one-on-ones-eb23bc24686a |
| "What to do in 1on1s" | Various | https://substack.com/home/post/p-172922961 |

**Key principles from this body of work:**
- The 1on1 belongs to the team member, not the manager.
- It is a trust-building space, not a status update.
- The manager's job is to ask questions, not solve problems.
- Topics that feel uncomfortable are often the most important ones.
- Recurring 1on1s build safety; cancelled or postponed 1on1s erode it.

## Radical Candor

| Source | Author | Reference |
|---|---|---|
| *Radical Candor* (book, 2017) | Kim Scott | https://www.radicalcandor.com/ |

**The 2x2 Quadrant:**

| | Challenges Directly ✅ | Challenges Directly ❌ |
|---|---|---|
| **Cares Personally ✅** | Radical Candor | Ruinous Empathy |
| **Cares Personally ❌** | Obnoxious Aggression | Manipulative Insincerity |

**Quadrant signals in transcripts:**
- **Radical Candor**: names a specific behaviour, explains the impact, shows care for the person — all in the same exchange.
- **Ruinous Empathy**: softens the message until the point is lost; avoids naming the problem; gives false reassurance; changes subject when things get uncomfortable.
- **Obnoxious Aggression**: direct, blunt, but without emotional acknowledgment; corrects or criticises without context or curiosity.
- **Manipulative Insincerity**: vague or uncommitted language; names a problem but makes no move toward resolution; false agreement to avoid conflict.

**Practical note:** Most managers default to Ruinous Empathy, not because they don't care, but because conflict feels unkind. The goal is not to become blunt — it's to care *and* challenge simultaneously.

## Feedback Quality — SBI Model

| Source | Author | URL |
|---|---|---|
| SBI Feedback Framework | Center for Creative Leadership (CCL) | https://www.ccl.org/articles/leading-effectively-articles/closing-the-gap-between-intent-vs-impact-sbii/ |

**Components:**
- **Situation (S):** The specific context or moment being referenced ("In yesterday's planning...")
- **Behaviour (B):** The concrete, observable action — not a trait, not an interpretation ("...you interrupted three times before the person finished...")
- **Impact (I):** The effect on the team, project, or relationship ("...which made it hard for others to contribute ideas.")

**Quality evaluation:**
- ✅ High quality: all three components present, behaviour is observable not judgmental.
- ⚠️ Medium quality: missing one component (e.g. impact implied but not stated).
- ❌ Low quality: jumps from situation to impact without naming the behaviour ("it went badly"); or names a trait instead of a behaviour ("you're disorganised").

**Also assess bidirectionality:** Did the team member have space to give feedback to the manager? Unidirectional feedback sessions are a missed opportunity and can signal low psychological safety.

## Psychological Safety

| Source | Author | Reference |
|---|---|---|
| *The Fearless Organization* (book, 2018) | Amy C. Edmondson | https://amycedmondson.com/psychological-safety/ |

**Definition:** Psychological safety is the belief that one can speak up, ask questions, admit mistakes, or raise concerns without being punished or humiliated.

**Signals of LOW psychological safety in transcripts:**
- Self-censorship markers: "no sé si debería decir esto", "igual me paso", "no sé...", "I'm not sure I should say this"
- Normalisation of harmful conditions: treating chronic blockers, unclear expectations, or absent leadership as just "how things are."
- Hedging before raising a concern: long preamble before getting to the actual issue.
- The team member minimises their own contributions or achievements.
- Absence of upward feedback — the team member never questions the manager's decisions or asks for clarity on manager behaviours.

**Signals of HIGH psychological safety:**
- The team member names problems directly and early.
- They disagree with the manager's framing and explain why.
- They ask for help without apologising for needing it.
- They share personal state (stress, uncertainty, frustration) without prompting.

## Conflict Management — Thomas-Kilmann Model

| Source | Author | URL |
|---|---|---|
| Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument | Kenneth Thomas & Ralph Kilmann (1974) | https://kilmanndiagnostics.com/overview-thomas-kilmann-conflict-mode-instrument-tki/ |

**Five conflict styles:**

| Style | Assertiveness | Cooperativeness | When useful | When harmful |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Collaborating** | High | High | Complex issues, long-term relationships | Too slow for urgent decisions |
| **Competing** | High | Low | Urgent, non-negotiable values | Damages relationships if overused |
| **Compromising** | Medium | Medium | Time-limited, balanced-power situations | May leave both parties unsatisfied |
| **Accommodating** | Low | High | Relationship preservation, minor issues | Breeds resentment over time |
| **Avoiding** | Low | Low | Trivial, cooling-off moments | Toxic for ongoing conflicts |

**Note for 1on1 analysis:** Avoiding is the most common and most damaging conflict style in management. Identifying it in a transcript is a high-signal improvement area.

## Coaching Style — Agency vs. Dependency

| Source | Author | Reference |
|---|---|---|
| *The Coaching Habit* (book, 2016) | Michael Bungay Stanier | https://boxofcrayons.com/the-coaching-habit-book/ |
| GROW Model | John Whitmore (1992) | https://coachingcouncil.com/grow-model/ |

**Agency:** The manager uses questions to help the team member think through their own situation and arrive at their own answers. The team member leaves with clarity and ownership.

**Dependency:** The manager diagnoses the problem and provides the solution. The team member becomes reliant on the manager for decisions and direction. This feels helpful but is often disempowering long-term.

**The GROW Model** as a lightweight coaching structure:
- **Goal** — What do you want to achieve?
- **Reality** — What's the current situation?
- **Options** — What could you do?
- **Will** — What will you do, and when?

**Key diagnostic question:** After the meeting, does the team member have *more clarity and energy*, or does the team member have *more tasks assigned by someone else*?

**The 7 coaching questions (Bungay Stanier):**
1. What's on your mind?
2. And what else?
3. What's the real challenge here for you?
4. What do you want?
5. How can I help?
6. If you're saying yes to this, what are you saying no to?
7. What was most useful for you?

## Career Development & Management

| Source | Author | Reference |
|---|---|---|
| *The Manager's Path* (book, 2017) | Camille Fournier | O'Reilly |
| *An Elegant Puzzle* (book, 2019) | Will Larson | Stripe Press |
| *First, Break All the Rules* (book, 1999) | Buckingham & Coffman | Gallup |
| *The Making of a Manager* (book, 2019) | Julie Zhuo | Portfolio/Penguin |
| *High Output Management* (book, 1983) | Andy Grove | Vintage |

**What good career development looks like in 1on1s:**
- The manager knows the team member's career goals (not just current project goals).
- There is an explicit or implicit IDP (Individual Development Plan): what skills to grow, in what timeframe, through what opportunities.
- The manager connects day-to-day work back to long-term growth ("this difficult situation is a chance to practice navigating ambiguity").
- The team member's trajectory is discussed at least once per quarter in dedicated time.

**Warning signals:**
- Career growth is never discussed, only current delivery or task output.
- The manager only talks about the team member's performance in terms of the team's results.
- The team member cannot name what they are trying to get better at.

## Talk Ratio & Active Listening

**Reference baseline:** In a well-conducted 1on1, the team member should speak approximately 60–70% of the time. The manager's role is to listen, probe, and occasionally reframe — not to transmit.

**Warning signals when manager talk ratio is high (>50%):**
- The manager is using the 1on1 to communicate updates, decisions, or feedback rather than to explore.
- The team member's topics and concerns are not being surfaced or are being cut short.
- The meeting dynamic is a briefing, not a conversation.

**How to estimate from a transcript:** Count approximate line volume per speaker across the full transcript. A rough line count is sufficient — exact word counts are not needed.

## Situational Leadership

| Source | Author | Reference |
|---|---|---|
| Situational Leadership Model | Paul Hersey & Ken Blanchard (1969) | https://situational.com/ |

**Four leadership styles matched to team member readiness:**

| Style | Directive | Supportive | For a team member who... |
|---|---|---|---|
| **S1 — Directing** | High | Low | Has low competence, high commitment (new to task or role) |
| **S2 — Coaching** | High | High | Has some competence, variable commitment |
| **S3 — Supporting** | Low | High | Has competence, but low confidence or motivation |
| **S4 — Delegating** | Low | Low | Is competent and committed — self-sufficient |

**Use in 1on1 analysis:** Detect mismatches between the team member's readiness level and the manager's leadership style. A common failure mode is applying S4 (hands-off) to team members who need S2 or S3 support.
